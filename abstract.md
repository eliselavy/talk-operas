"Thinkering” with Digital Research Infrastructures in Humanities

Authors: Tugce Karatas , Valérie Schafer, Sofia Papastamkou, Elisabeth Guerard
Abstract

As Mounier and Dumas Primbault (2023) claim in Sustaining Knowledge and Governing Its Infrastructure in the Digital Age: An Integrated View, "knowledge infrastructures are at the centre of issues related to openness." Indeed, they are not neutral enablers of Open Science (OS). Providing technical solutions for access, searchability, interoperability, and sustainability involves continuous negotiation, friction, and a careful balance of skills, power, and human, technical, and economic resources. Therefore, tensions are inherent to the shaping of knowledge infrastructures—if not a driving force, as Hanseth et al. (2021) demonstrate. This paper adopts a critical perspective, arguing that research infrastructures are inherently normative socio-technical formations that actively shape scholarly practices, epistemic values, and power relations in the Humanities. Drawing on empirical experiences with digital research infrastructures spanning the entire data lifecycle—from production and processing through to archiving, preservation, and digitisation in a centre for digital history—it examines how infrastructural choices such as data models, openness policies, governance structures, and evaluation mechanisms may privilege certain disciplinary assumptions while marginalising others. By foregrounding issues of disciplinary maturity, authorship, and openness, this paper highlights how infrastructures function as boundary objects (Star and Griesemer, 1989) that enable cooperation without consensus while simultaneously producing forms of selection and curation. A core case study concerns the choice of a solution enabling interoperability across several repositories, infrastructures, and data lakes at an academic centre. This involves complex negotiations and critical methodological choices across diverse disciplinary approaches. The repositories were created over several years, each with distinct genealogies, preservation levels, and metadata standards. They contain materials with diverse authorship rights and origins. Making them accessible, retrievable, and searchable is not merely a technical matter. Several critical questions arise: Do all potential users require the same level of serendipity and information access? Should the centre privilege an internally developed bespoke tool or a proprietary solution designed by a company which relies on a different business model and could provide a ready-made solution with regular updates, but offers less control over data and creates potential path dependencies? How can convergence be achieved when dealing with diverse needs, skill levels, interests, and requests? This situation calls for reflexive practices. Scholar-governed infrastructures are only possible if the community recognises its political and epistemic agency and actively engages with the polyvocality of Humanities research to pursue a more equitable OS ecosystem. By accommodating exploratory, interpretive, and highly curated research within the same ecosystem, scholar-driven infrastructures can align FAIR principles with Humanities values, supporting reuse, care (Denis and Pontille, 2022), long-term preservation, and trust without erasing disciplinary specificity. However, this requires discussions and strategies that may be described as "thinkering"—a portmanteau of "thinking" and "tinkering" coined by Michael Ondaatje in The English Patient. The paper analyses this ongoing trial-and-error process of benchmarking, parallel exploration of internal and external options, creation of a users' group, and stakeholder engagement, illustrating how tensions "should be leveraged for their contributions to long-term properties of infrastructural fit, equity, and sustainability" (Edwards et al., 2007, p. 28).
